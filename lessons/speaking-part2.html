Test Format

What is Speaking Part 2?

The Basics

  • Also called the Long Turn or Individual Long Turn
  • You receive a cue card with a topic and bullet points
  • You have 1 minute to prepare — you are given a pencil and paper
  • You then speak for 1–2 minutes without interruption
  • The examiner may ask 1–2 follow-up questions at the end
  • The whole Part 2 section lasts about 3–4 minutes

What the Cue Card Looks Like

Describe a place you have visited that you found particularly interesting.
You should say:
  • where the place is
  • when you went there
  • what you did there
  • and explain why you found it particularly interesting
You will have one minute to prepare before speaking.

Key Rules

  • You must speak for at least 1 minute — stopping early is penalised
  • Aim for the full 2 minutes — the examiner will tell you to stop
  • You should cover all the bullet points on the cue card
  • You can invent or exaggerate details — this is NOT tested for honesty
  • You are marked on the same 4 criteria as Parts 1 and 3
⚠ Do NOT ask the examiner questions during your talk ⚠ Do NOT stop and say "I've finished" — keep talking until told to stop ✓ Making notes during prep time is strongly recommended
2
Preparation

Making the Most of Your 1 Minute

What to Write in Your Notes

Use key words and short phrases — not full sentences. Your notes are a reminder, not a script. Focus on:

  • A specific person/place/event to talk about (be concrete)
  • One idea per bullet point on the cue card
  • 1–2 good vocabulary words you want to use
  • How you will end — the "explain" or "why" bullet

How to Choose Your Topic

Always choose something specific and concrete, not vague. A specific choice gives you much more to say.

  • ✗ "a place I visited" → ✓ "the Louvre in Paris in 2019"
  • ✗ "a book I read" → ✓ "1984 by George Orwell at age 17"
  • ✗ "a person I admire" → ✓ "my secondary school English teacher, Mr. Ali"

Sample Prep Notes (30 seconds)

Topic: Interesting place → Chichen Itza, Mexico, 2022
Where: Yucatan peninsula, southern Mexico
When: March 2022, family trip
What: climbed pyramid, saw ball court, guided tour
Why: ancient Mayan civilisation — scale, mystery, unexpected
Key words: awe-inspiring, crumbling, civilisation, remnants
Can I choose a topic I know nothing about? You can invent everything — the place, the person, the event. Pick something you can talk about confidently. The examiner will never check. Choose a topic that lets you show rich vocabulary and varied grammar.
3
Talk Structure

How to Structure Your 2-Minute Talk

Use the PEEL method as a loose framework. It keeps your talk organised and ensures you cover everything.

P
Point — Introduce Your Topic (15–20 seconds)

State what you're going to talk about clearly. Briefly introduce the who/what/where. "I'd like to talk about…" or "The place I want to describe is…"

E
Explain — Work Through the Bullet Points (60–70 seconds)

Cover each bullet point on the cue card in turn. Give specific details, examples, and descriptions. This is the main body of your talk.

E
Elaborate — Add Personal Feelings / Reactions (20–25 seconds)

Expand beyond the bullet points with your personal response. How did you feel? What surprised you? What stood out? This is where strong vocabulary shines.

L
Link — Conclude or Reflect (10–15 seconds)

Round off your talk with a brief reflection. "Looking back…", "What I take from this is…", "It's an experience I'd highly recommend because…"

Useful Phrases for Each Stage

  • Introduction: "I'd like to talk about…" / "The [topic] I'm going to describe is…" / "This is something I feel strongly about because…"
  • Covering bullet points: "In terms of [bullet]…" / "As far as [bullet] is concerned…" / "When it comes to [bullet]…"
  • Elaborating: "What really struck me was…" / "I was particularly impressed by…" / "Looking back, I think what made it special was…"
  • Concluding: "All in all…" / "To sum up…" / "It's definitely an experience I'd recommend because…"
✓ Use "In terms of…" to move from one bullet to the next smoothly ⚠ Don't just list facts — include feelings and opinions too
4
Language

Useful Language for Part 2

Describing Places

  • breathtaking / awe-inspiring / stunning
  • bustling / vibrant / lively / tranquil / serene
  • well-preserved / crumbling / renovated / abandoned
  • off the beaten track / touristy / unspoiled
  • surrounded by / nestled in / perched on / overlooking

Describing People

  • dedicated / passionate / inspiring / compassionate
  • down-to-earth / approachable / outspoken / reserved
  • had a lasting impact on / influenced / shaped
  • looked up to / admired / respected
  • went out of their way to / made a point of

Expressing Feelings & Reactions

  • I was completely taken aback by…
  • It left a lasting impression on me
  • I was moved / overwhelmed / blown away
  • It was quite unlike anything I'd experienced before
  • I hadn't anticipated how… it would be
  • It gave me a real sense of…

Extending Your Talk

  • What made it even more special was…
  • Another aspect worth mentioning is…
  • It's also worth noting that…
  • I think what I'll always remember is…
  • Come to think of it…
  • I suppose what struck me most was…

Practice

Cue Card Practice

How to Practise Read the cue card, take exactly 1 minute to make notes, then speak for 2 minutes. Record yourself if possible. After speaking, compare your answer to the model answer below.
Describe a memorable journey or trip you have taken.
You should say:
  • where you went
  • who you went with
  • what you did during the trip
  • and explain why the journey was memorable
You will have one minute to prepare before speaking.
Show Model Answer & Key Vocabulary

"I'd like to talk about a trip I took to Istanbul a few years ago with two of my closest friends. It was actually a spontaneous decision — we booked the flights just two weeks before — which made the whole experience feel even more exciting.

In terms of what we did, we spent most of the time exploring the old city — visiting the Hagia Sophia, wandering through the Grand Bazaar, and taking a boat trip along the Bosphorus. What really struck me was the sheer contrast between the modern city and its ancient history. In the morning we'd be walking through a 1,500-year-old cathedral, and by the afternoon we were sitting in a trendy rooftop cafe overlooking two continents.

Looking back, I think what made the trip so memorable was the spontaneity of it. We had no fixed plan, which meant we stumbled across things we'd never have found on a guided tour — tiny spice markets, hidden courtyards, local restaurants with incredible food. I was completely taken aback by how welcoming the city was. It's definitely a place I'd return to."


  • Key vocab: spontaneous, sheer contrast, stumbled across, welcoming, trendy, wandering
  • Structure: Introduced the trip → covered all bullet points → elaborated with personal reaction → concluded
  • Grammar used: past perfect ("we'd never have found"), conditional ("I'd return"), relative clauses
Describe a person who has had a significant influence on your life.
You should say:
  • who this person is
  • how you know them
  • what this person has done
  • and explain why they have had such a significant influence on you
You will have one minute to prepare before speaking.
Show Model Answer & Key Vocabulary

"The person I'd like to talk about is my secondary school English teacher, whose name was Ms. Kowalska. I first met her when I was around 14 years old, and she taught me for three years.

What set her apart from other teachers was her genuine passion for literature and language. She didn't just teach us grammar rules — she made us fall in love with reading by choosing books that were actually relevant to our lives. I remember she introduced us to George Orwell's '1984', which completely changed the way I thought about language and power.

In terms of her influence on me personally, I think she's the main reason I pursued languages seriously. She had this remarkable ability to make every student feel capable. When I was struggling with writing essays, she stayed after class on multiple occasions just to help me improve — she went completely out of her way for her students.

All in all, I think the greatest gift she gave me was confidence in my own voice. I wouldn't be where I am today without her encouragement."


  • Key vocab: genuine passion, set her apart, went out of her way, remarkable ability, confidence in my own voice
  • Tenses used: past simple, past continuous, present perfect ("wouldn't be where I am")
Describe a skill you have learned that you consider useful.
You should say:
  • what the skill is
  • how and when you learned it
  • how often you use it
  • and explain why you consider it useful
You will have one minute to prepare before speaking.
Show Model Answer & Key Vocabulary

"The skill I'd like to describe is public speaking — specifically, the ability to present ideas clearly and confidently in front of an audience. I started developing this skill in my first year at university, when I was required to give presentations as part of my coursework. To be honest, I was absolutely terrified at first.

I improved mainly by doing it repeatedly — joining a debating club, volunteering to present at team meetings, watching videos of skilled speakers to understand how they structured their arguments and used pauses for effect. It was a slow process, but over time I noticed a real transformation.

These days I use this skill on a fairly regular basis — in work presentations, in negotiations, and even in social situations where I need to express an idea persuasively. What I find most valuable about it is that it's transferable to almost every area of life. In today's world, being able to communicate clearly and confidently is arguably as important as any technical qualification."


  • Key vocab: transferable, persuasively, transformation, arguably, confidently, repeatedly
  • Good phrases: "over time I noticed", "to be honest", "what I find most valuable"

Practice & Resources

Examiner Tips

  • speak fluently and without hesitation,
  • Be fluent and liberated Speak fluently and spontaneously. You will gain more points. Don't worry too much about using clever vocabulary, it's more important to be fluent. But also don't speak too quick and mind your grammar. You should find a "healthy balance" between speaking too quickly and making long pauses.
  • Practise answering sample questions Typically, you will be asked about everyday topics, such as work, studies, sport, family and so on. So you should try answering IELTS Speaking questions before the exam. You will be surprised how simple they are! You just need to learn appropriate vocabulary and understand what answers you will give.
  • Ask the question again if you need to Don't be shy, if you want to clarify something. You will not lose points for asking the examiner.
  • Be emotional! Speak with emotions. Nothing separates the experienced speaker from beginners as tone of the speech. Express your feelings like you would do using your native language.
  • Be coherent Use linking words and structures. Words and phrases like however , nevertheless , all in all , moreover will enrich your speech.
