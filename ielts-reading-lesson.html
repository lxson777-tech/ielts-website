1
True / False / Not Given

True, False, Not Given

What is it?

You read a statement and decide whether the passage confirms it (TRUE), contradicts it (FALSE), or simply doesn't mention it at all (NOT GIVEN). The key is that NOT GIVEN means the passage is silent — not that the statement is wrong.

Key Distinctions

  • TRUE — the statement matches the passage information
  • FALSE — the statement directly contradicts the passage
  • NOT GIVEN — there is no information to decide either way

How to Approach It

  1. Read each statement carefully and identify keywords.
  2. Scan the passage to locate the relevant section (answers appear in order).
  3. Read that section and the sentences around it — not just one line.
  4. Focus on meaning, not just word-matching. The passage will paraphrase the statement.
  5. Be especially careful with NOT GIVEN — ask: "does the passage give any information about this at all?"
⚠ Trap: "believed" ≠ factual truth ⚠ Trap: numbers without context (increase? decrease?) ⚠ Trap: idiomatic expressions — read for meaning
Passage: Spam Messaging

SPAM, as every user of mobile phones in China is aware to their intense annoyance, is a roaring trade in China. Its delivery-men drive through residential neighbourhoods in "text-messaging cars", with illegal but easy-to-buy gadgetry they use to hijack links between mobile-phone users and nearby communications masts. They then target the numbers they harvest, blasting them with spam text messages before driving away. Mobile-phone users usually see only the wearisome results: another sprinkling of spam messages offering deals on flats, investment advice and dodgy receipts for tax purposes.

Chinese mobile-users get more spam text messages than their counterparts anywhere else in the world. They received slightly more than 300 billion of them in 2013, or close to one a day for each person using a mobile phone. Users in bigger markets like Beijing and Shanghai receive two a day, or more than 700 annually, accounting for perhaps one-fifth to one-third of all texts. Americans, by comparison, received an estimated 4.5 billion junk messages in 2011, or fewer than 20 per mobile-user for the year—out of a total of more than two trillion text messages sent.

Exercise — Decide: True, False, or Not Given

  1. In China, SPAM text messaging is a successful business.
  2. People's phone numbers are collected through technology which cannot be readily bought.
  3. In no other country do people receive more spam texts than in China.
  4. In 2013, the number of SPAM texts increased considerably to reach at least 300 billion.
  5. The majority of all texts received in Shanghai and Beijing are SPAM.
  6. In 2011, Americans sent more texts than anywhere else in the world.
Show Answers & Explanations
  1. TRUE — "a roaring trade" = a successful/booming business
  2. FALSE — the passage says the gadgetry is "easy-to-buy", which directly contradicts "cannot be readily bought"
  3. TRUE — "Chinese mobile-users get more spam…than their counterparts anywhere else in the world"
  4. NOT GIVEN — the passage gives the 2013 figure but never says whether it increased from a previous year
  5. FALSE — spam accounts for one-fifth to one-third at most, not the majority
  6. NOT GIVEN — the passage mentions how many Americans received spam, not total texts sent vs. other countries

Multiple Choice

Multiple Choice Questions

What is it?

You are given a question or an incomplete sentence with 3 answer options (A, B, C). You must locate the information in the passage and choose the single correct option. Always write a letter as your answer — never words.

Two Forms

  • Form A: A question to answer — e.g. "What is the main reason…?"
  • Form B: A sentence to complete — e.g. "Rice is difficult to grow because…"

Both require the same skill: locate and understand meaning.

How to Approach It

  1. Read the question and all three options carefully before looking at the passage.
  2. Identify differences between the options — they may be subtly different.
  3. Prepare paraphrases and synonyms for the question keywords.
  4. Scan the passage for the relevant section (answers come in order).
  5. Read the surrounding sentences — not just the one that matches.
  6. You may see information about all three options in the passage — only one is correct.
  7. Choose based on deeper meaning, not just word-matching.
⚠ Trap: all options may appear in the passage — only one is accurate ⚠ Trap: similar-sounding options with small but crucial differences
Passage: All About Rice

The first rice may have been grown in East and South Asia as long as 15,000 years ago, when people began to settle in river deltas and domesticated wild rice. Today it is grown practically everywhere, except Antarctica! Rice is grown on flooded land and on dry land, in tropical rain forests of Africa and in arid deserts of the Middle East, on coastal plains and on the Himalayan mountains.

In the year 2003, the world produced about 589 million tonnes of paddy rice. Asia produces about 90% of the world's rice. However, Asian countries also consume most of what they produce. By comparison, the United States grows only about 1% of the world's rice, but it exports around half of what it grows and is one of the world's leading exporters.

Rice is a key source of nutrition in developing nations and is the dietary staple for half the world's population. Unlike wheat, which is grown mainly for its grain, rice plants are used for many purposes: the grain is eaten, the straw is used for animal feed, thatching, and weaving, and the husks are used for fuel and fertiliser. Farmers in some parts of Asia have grown rice in the same fields for 2,000 years without harming the soil.

Exercise — Choose the correct answer A, B, or C

  1. When was rice first cultivated? A) Around 15,000 years ago B) When people first came to Asia C) After people settled in mountains
  2. Which country is one of the world's leading rice exporters? A) China B) India C) The United States
  3. How is the rice plant different from wheat? A) It produces more grain per harvest B) Multiple parts of the plant are used C) It does not need water to grow
Show Answers & Explanations
  1. A — "as long as 15,000 years ago" is directly stated in the passage
  2. C — the passage states the US "is one of the world's leading exporters" despite growing only 1%
  3. B — "rice plants are used for many purposes" (grain, straw, husks) unlike wheat which is grown mainly for its grain

Diagram Labelling

Diagram / Picture Labelling

What is it?

You are given a diagram (a drawing, map, or technical figure) with numbered blank labels. You must find the correct word(s) from the passage to fill each blank. The answer is always a word or words taken directly from the passage.

Key Rules

  • You must use words from the passage — do not paraphrase
  • Pay attention to the word limit: usually "no more than two words"
  • Spelling must be correct
  • The diagram shows a visual — your job is to connect parts of the image to terminology in the text

How to Approach It

  1. Study the diagram first. What is it showing? What parts are labelled and what are blank?
  2. Read the passage and identify the section that describes this diagram.
  3. Match each blank to the position on the diagram — think about location/function.
  4. Find the exact word(s) in the passage that name that part.
  5. Check the word limit — never exceed it.
⚠ Trap: writing paraphrases instead of exact passage words ⚠ Trap: exceeding the word limit ⚠ Trap: misspelling technical terms

Passage: About Ants

There are more than 10,000 known species of ants living throughout the world. Ants can be identified by the constriction that looks like a waist between the abdomen, at the end of the body, and the part of the body called the thorax, which is located before the head. Their bodies are covered with a hard armour called the exoskeleton. While the exoskeleton is their defensive weapon, their rear abdominal stinger is their offensive one. Like other insects, they have six legs; each with three joints. Ants have large heads with compound eyes, elbowed antennae, which act as sensors, and powerful pincers, known as mandibles. These pincers are an ant's most useful tool which are used for grabbing things, cutting and are a kind of trap-jaw.

There are three types of adult ants living in the colony: the queen, female ants, and male ants. Queen ants are the largest ants in the colony. They have wings and their purpose is to lay eggs. The queens can live up to 30 years. The ant life cycle has four stages of development: egg, larva, pupa and adult. The queen lays the eggs, which will hatch into larvae in a couple of days. The helpless larva are fed and groomed by the worker ants until they pass through the pupal stage. In a little more than a week, an adult ant will emerge and the metamorphosis is complete.

Exercise — Diagram Completion (use NO MORE than 2 words from the passage)

Label the following parts of the ant's body using words from the passage above:

  1. The hard covering protecting the ant's body → _________
  2. The middle section of the body, between head and abdomen → _________
  3. The sensory organs on the head, which are elbowed → _________
  4. The powerful biting tools on the head, also called pincers → _________
  5. The rear weapon used for attack → _________

Bonus True/False/NG:

  1. The antennae of an ant cannot bend.
  2. Only queen ants have wings.
  3. Larvae undergo metamorphosis in the pupal stage.
Show Answers
  1. exoskeleton
  2. thorax
  3. antennae
  4. mandibles
  5. stinger (or "abdominal stinger")
  6. FALSE — the passage says antennae are "elbowed" (meaning they do bend)
  7. TRUE — only queen ants are described as having wings
  8. TRUE — "they pass through the pupal stage" and "metamorphosis is complete"

Matching Headings

Matching Headings

What is it?

You are given a list of headings (roman numerals: i, ii, iii…) and a passage with labelled paragraphs (A, B, C…). You must choose the best heading for each paragraph. There are always more headings than paragraphs — some headings won't be used.

Important Distinction

This is NOT Matching Paragraph Information. A heading is a title — it must describe the whole paragraph's main idea, not just one detail inside it. One matching detail ≠ the correct heading.

How to Approach It

  1. Read through all the headings first and note synonyms/paraphrases.
  2. For each paragraph, read to find its central aim — what is the paragraph mainly about?
  3. Distinguish between the main idea and supporting examples or details.
  4. Eliminate headings that only match one sentence in the paragraph.
  5. Watch for headings that look similar — compare them carefully.
  6. Your answer must be a roman numeral (e.g. III).
⚠ Trap: choosing a heading that matches one detail, not the whole paragraph ⚠ Trap: headings with similar wording — look at meaning, not just words
Passage: The Greenhouse Effect

A. A greenhouse is a house made entirely of glass. One of the main purposes of a greenhouse is to grow plants that might struggle outside. A greenhouse stays warm inside, even during winter. Sunlight shines in and warms the plants and air inside. But the heat is trapped by the glass and cannot escape. So during the daylight hours, it gets warmer inside a greenhouse, and stays quite warm at night too.

B. The Earth experiences a similar thing to a greenhouse. Gases in the atmosphere such as carbon dioxide do what the roof of a greenhouse does. During the day, the Sun shines through the atmosphere and Earth's surface warms up. At night, Earth's surface cools, releasing heat back into the air. But some of the heat is trapped by the greenhouse gases. That is what keeps our Earth a comfortable temperature on average.

C. However, humans have been adding extra greenhouse gases — especially carbon dioxide — to the atmosphere. When we burn fossil fuels such as coal and oil, we release CO₂. These extra gases are trapping more heat than usual, causing global temperatures to rise. Scientists call this enhanced greenhouse effect the cause of global warming.

D. What can we do? Many governments are working on agreements to reduce emissions. Individuals can make changes too: driving less, using renewable energy, and eating less meat all help reduce the amount of carbon dioxide we put into the atmosphere. Our everyday choices have a direct impact on the future of the planet.

Exercise — Choose the correct heading (I–IX) for paragraphs A–D

  • I — Changing temperatures
  • II — The greenhouse structure
  • III — Global warming
  • IV — Use of a greenhouse for plants
  • V — Scientific research findings
  • VI — Earth's atmosphere and natural warmth
  • VII — Our choices
  • VIII — Effects of burning fossil fuels
  • IX — Climates around the world
  1. Paragraph A → _____
  2. Paragraph B → _____
  3. Paragraph C → _____
  4. Paragraph D → _____
Show Answers & Explanations
  1. II — The greenhouse structure — the whole paragraph describes what a greenhouse is and how it works physically
  2. VI — Earth's atmosphere and natural warmth — the paragraph explains how the Earth's atmosphere naturally traps heat, like a greenhouse
  3. VIII — Effects of burning fossil fuels — the main idea is that human emissions enhance the greenhouse effect and cause warming
  4. VII — Our choices — the paragraph is entirely about what individuals and governments can do

Note: Heading III (Global warming) might seem right for C, but VIII is more precise about the cause discussed in the paragraph.

Sentence Completion

Sentence Completion

What is it?

You are given incomplete sentences with a gap (___). You must find the correct word(s) from the passage to complete each sentence. You must use exact words from the passage and stay within the given word limit (usually "no more than three words and/or a number").

Key Rules

  • Words must come directly from the passage
  • Obey the word limit strictly — 4 words when limit is 3 = wrong
  • Spelling must be correct
  • Answers follow the order of the passage

How to Approach It

  1. Read each incomplete sentence and identify keywords before searching.
  2. Think about what type of word is missing (noun, verb, adjective, number?).
  3. Scan the passage using keywords and synonyms to locate the relevant section.
  4. Read carefully around that section and identify the exact word(s) that complete the sentence logically and grammatically.
  5. Write the answer — check spelling and word count.
⚠ Trap: paraphrasing instead of copying exact words ⚠ Trap: going over the word limit ⚠ Trap: ignoring grammar — the completed sentence must make grammatical sense
Passage: Pyramid Building

The most famous pyramid is the Great Pyramid of Giza which is actually only one of over a hundred surviving pyramids. Scientists are currently piecing together the puzzle of how they were built over 4,000 years ago. The blocks which make up the pyramids were hewn from quarries and then transported to the pyramids for construction. The transportation of the materials was either by river using a boat or by land using a wooden sledge. It is believed that the sand in front of the sledge was wet with water in order to facilitate the movement of the sledge and reduce friction. These sledges were pulled manually or sometimes by using beasts of burden depending on the ease at which the sledges could move over the ground.

While popular belief is that the Great Pyramid was built using slave labour, this theory has since been debunked. The first building made in a pyramid shape is thought to be the Stepped Pyramid, which consists of six steps placed on top of each other. The credit for finally achieving a smooth-sided pyramid goes to Imhotep, an architect commissioned by King Sneferu. The pyramids were not an instant achievement, but the achievement of trial and error.

Exercise — Complete the sentences using NO MORE than THREE words from the passage

  1. Scientists are still trying to understand how the pyramids were built; they describe this as ________________ the puzzle.
  2. Pyramid blocks were transported either by river or by land using a ________________.
  3. To reduce friction, the sand in front of the sledge was made wet ________________.
  4. Sledges were pulled either by hand or using ________________.
  5. The theory that slave labour was used to build the pyramids has been ________________.
  6. The architect who achieved the first smooth-sided pyramid was ________________, working for King Sneferu.
Show Answers
  1. piecing together
  2. wooden sledge
  3. with water
  4. beasts of burden
  5. debunked
  6. Imhotep

Matching Paragraphs

Matching Paragraph Information

What is it?

You are given a list of statements. Each statement contains a specific piece of information. You must identify which labelled paragraph (A, B, C…) contains that information. This is about locating specific details — not the main idea of the paragraph.

vs. Matching Headings

  • Matching Headings → What is the paragraph mainly about? (the whole)
  • Matching Paragraphs → Which paragraph contains this specific detail?
  • One paragraph can be the answer to more than one question
  • Your answer is always a letter

How to Approach It

  1. Read all the statements first and identify keywords and paraphrases.
  2. Skim the passage to get a sense of what each paragraph covers.
  3. For each statement, scan the passage for keywords or synonyms.
  4. When you find the relevant section, confirm it contains the information in the statement.
  5. Remember: a paragraph can answer more than one question — check the instructions.
⚠ Trap: confusing this with Matching Headings ⚠ Trap: forgetting that one letter can be used more than once
Passage: Beethoven

A. Composer Ludwig van Beethoven was born on or near December 16, 1770, in Bonn, Germany. He is widely considered the greatest composer of all time. Sometime between the births of his two younger brothers, Beethoven's father began teaching him music with an extraordinary rigour and brutality, which affected him for the rest of his life. On a near daily basis, Beethoven was flogged, locked in the cellar and deprived of sleep for extra hours of practice.

B. In 1804, Beethoven was completing his third symphony, which he at first named "Bonaparte" in honour of Napoleon. However, when later that year Napoleon proclaimed himself Emperor, Beethoven was so disappointed that he renamed his composition "Eroica". This work debuted in Vienna in 1805. It was so unlike anything heard before that through weeks of rehearsal, the musicians could not figure out how to play it.

C. At the same time as he was composing these great works, Beethoven was trying hard to come to terms with a shocking fact that he tried desperately to conceal. He was going deaf. At the turn of the century, Beethoven struggled to make out the words spoken to him in conversation.

D. Despite his extraordinary output of beautiful music, Beethoven was frequently miserable throughout his adult life. Beethoven died on March 26, 1827, at the age of 56.

Exercise — Which paragraph (A, B, C, or D) contains the following information?

  1. A reference to the harsh methods used to teach Beethoven music as a child
  2. A mention of Beethoven concealing a personal problem
  3. Information about a work that was initially impossible for musicians to perform
  4. The composer's date of death
  5. A reference to Beethoven's disappointment in a political figure
  6. Details about Beethoven's difficulty communicating with others
Show Answers
  1. A — "extraordinary rigour and brutality… flogged, locked in the cellar"
  2. C — "trying desperately to conceal… he was going deaf"
  3. B — "musicians could not figure out how to play it"
  4. D — "Beethoven died on March 26, 1827"
  5. B — "Napoleon proclaimed himself Emperor, Beethoven was so disappointed"
  6. C — "struggled to make out the words spoken to him in conversation"

Categorisation / Classification

Categorisation Questions

What is it?

Also called Classification or Matching Features. You are given a list of statements and a set of 2–3 categories (A, B, C). You must decide which category each statement belongs to, based on information in the passage. Multiple statements can share the same category.

Key Rules

  • Your answer is always a letter
  • Multiple questions can have the same letter as an answer
  • The categories are often people, places, time periods, or groups
  • The passage decides which category — use only what's in the text

How to Approach It

  1. Read the categories carefully — understand what each one represents.
  2. Skim the passage to identify which section refers to each category.
  3. Read each statement and identify keywords.
  4. Locate the relevant passage section and decide which category the information belongs to.
  5. Don't panic if the same letter appears several times — that's normal.
⚠ Trap: using general knowledge — rely only on the passage ⚠ Trap: assuming each category is used only once
Passage: Three Ancient Civilisations

The Egyptians were one of the most technologically advanced ancient civilisations. They developed a writing system called hieroglyphics, built massive stone monuments including the pyramids, and created a complex system of mathematics and astronomy. Their society was ruled by a god-king known as a Pharaoh, and religion was central to daily life. Embalming the dead and building elaborate tombs were key practices.

The Greeks made extraordinary contributions to philosophy, science, and democracy. Greek thinkers such as Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle developed ideas that still influence modern thought. The Greeks introduced the concept of democracy in Athens, constructed architectural masterpieces like the Parthenon, and founded the Olympic Games as a religious and athletic festival.

The Romans were famous for their military power and engineering skill. They built an extensive network of roads that connected their empire, constructed aqueducts to supply water to cities, and developed a legal system that forms the basis of many modern laws. Roman culture absorbed many elements of Greek philosophy and religion, and Latin — the Roman language — is the root of several modern European languages.

Exercise — Classify each statement as belonging to:

A = Egyptians  |  B = Greeks  |  C = Romans

  1. This civilisation introduced a system of government involving citizens in decision-making.
  2. This civilisation built a road network across their empire.
  3. This civilisation developed a written language using pictures and symbols.
  4. This civilisation founded a sporting competition with religious significance.
  5. This civilisation created a legal framework that influenced modern law.
  6. This civilisation used a process to preserve the bodies of the dead.
  7. This civilisation's language is the origin of many modern European languages.
  8. This civilisation built structures to transport water into urban areas.
Show Answers
  1. B — Greeks introduced democracy in Athens
  2. C — Romans built an extensive road network
  3. A — Egyptians developed hieroglyphics
  4. B — Greeks founded the Olympic Games
  5. C — Romans developed a legal system
  6. A — Egyptians practised embalming
  7. C — Latin is the root of modern European languages
  8. C — Romans constructed aqueducts
