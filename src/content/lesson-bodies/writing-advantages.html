Advantages & Disadvantages Essays

What is it?

The prompt describes a trend or practice and asks about its benefits and drawbacks. It comes in two different forms that need different essays — read the exact wording before you plan. (The four-paragraph method itself is covered in How to Answer Task 2 — this lesson is about adapting it to advantages/disadvantages questions.)

Which Form Do You Have?

  • Neutral form: "What are the advantages and disadvantages of this?" — no opinion required, just balanced analysis.
  • Opinion form: "Do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?" — you must also state and defend a verdict.
  • Mistaking one for the other is the single most common error on this essay type — a neutral essay with a hidden opinion, or an opinion essay that never actually gives one, both lose Task Response marks.

Structure (4 paragraphs — one detail per adaptation)

  1. Introduction: paraphrase the topic. Neutral form: preview that you'll examine both sides. Opinion form: also state your verdict here — "While there are some benefits to this trend, I believe the disadvantages are more significant."
  2. Body 1 — Advantages: your strongest 1–2 benefits, each with TEE (Topic → Explanation → Example).
  3. Body 2 — Disadvantages: your strongest 1–2 drawbacks, same TEE shape and the same length as Body 1 — don't let one side dominate just because it's the one you agree with.
  4. Conclusion: neutral form summarises both sides evenly; opinion form restates your verdict, weighing the two against each other.

Some teachers give the opinion form a 5th paragraph — a short "my view" paragraph after the two body paragraphs — instead of folding the verdict into the introduction and conclusion only. Either is safe as long as the position is unmistakable throughout.

JobLanguage

Example question (opinion form)

Many parents now buy smartphones for children at a young age. Do the advantages of this outweigh the disadvantages?
Show model introduction & conclusion

It has become increasingly common for parents to give children mobile phones from an early age. While this practice offers genuine safety and educational benefits, I would argue that the risks to children's wellbeing and development make the disadvantages more significant overall.

In conclusion, although early phone ownership can keep children safe and connected, the risks of excessive screen time and exposure to inappropriate content are, on balance, the more serious consideration. Parents who choose to provide a phone should therefore pair it with clear rules on its use.


  • Opinion form correctly identified from "do the advantages outweigh" ✓
  • Verdict clear in the introduction and unchanged in the conclusion ✓
  • Conclusion weighs both sides against each other, not just restates them ✓

Common Mistakes

⚠ Writing a neutral essay when the question asked "do the advantages outweigh" — no verdict given ⚠ Sneaking a personal opinion into a neutral-form essay that didn't ask for one ⚠ Giving four rushed one-line points instead of two well-developed ones per side ⚠ Devoting three sentences to advantages and one to disadvantages (or vice versa)
