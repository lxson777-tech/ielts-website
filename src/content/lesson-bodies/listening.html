Test Format

How the Listening Test Works

The Basics

  • Total time: approximately 30 minutes (+ 10 mins transfer time)
  • 40 questions across 4 sections
  • Each recording is played once only — no second chance
  • Recordings increase in difficulty from Section 1 to Section 4
  • You write answers on the question paper, then transfer to the answer sheet

Test Structure

The Four Sections

Section 1 — Easiest

  • Type: Conversation between two people
  • Context: Everyday social/transactional situation (booking, enquiry, registration)
  • Example: A person calling to book a hotel, or someone registering at a sports club
  • Typical question type: Form / note completion
  • Usually involves names, numbers, addresses, times

Section 2 — Easy-Medium

  • Type: Monologue (one speaker)
  • Context: Non-academic social context (a talk, a tour, a public announcement)
  • Example: A guide describing a local facility, or a radio presenter describing an event
  • Typical question types: Map labelling, multiple choice, matching

Section 3 — Medium-Hard

  • Type: Conversation between 2–4 speakers
  • Context: Educational / training setting
  • Example: Students discussing an assignment, or a student and a tutor
  • Typical question types: Multiple choice, matching, sentence completion
  • Requires tracking multiple speakers and paraphrased ideas

Section 4 — Hardest

  • Type: Academic monologue (one speaker)
  • Context: University lecture or academic talk
  • Example: A lecture on climate change, urban development, or psychology
  • Typical question types: Note / summary completion, sentence completion
  • Dense academic language, fast pace, no pauses
3
Question Types

Listening Question Types

Form / Note Completion

What: Fill in blanks in a form or set of notes. Words must come from the recording.

Watch for: Word limits ("no more than two words and/or a number"). Spelling of names and addresses. Numbers — listen carefully for "fifteen" vs "fifty".

Multiple Choice

What: Choose A, B, or C. The question may have 3 options or ask you to choose multiple correct answers.

Watch for: Distractors — the speaker may mention all three options but only one is correct. Listen for correction language: "actually…", "I mean…", "no, wait…".

Matching

What: Match a list of items (e.g. events) to a list of features or descriptions. There are always more features than items.

Watch for: Paraphrasing — the recording will not use the same words as the options. Focus on meaning, not exact words.

Map / Diagram Labelling

What: Fill in blank labels on a map or floor plan. Words come from the recording.

Watch for: Location language — "opposite", "next to", "between", "on the corner of", "turn left at". Study the map layout before the recording starts.

Sentence / Summary Completion

What: Complete sentences or a paragraph summary using words from the recording. Word limit applies.

Watch for: The sentence must be grammatically correct with your answer inserted. This helps you anticipate the correct word type (noun, adjective, verb). Answers follow the order of the recording.

⚠ All question types: answers appear in the order they are heard ✓ Use the reading time to predict what type of information is needed
4
Strategy

How to Approach the Listening Test

Before Listening — Read Ahead

  • Use every second of reading time to preview the questions
  • Underline keywords in each question
  • Predict what type of information is needed: a name? a number? a place?
  • For form completion: predict the format (e.g. this blank needs a date)
  • For multiple choice: read all options and identify how they differ

While Listening

  • Follow the questions in order — answers come in sequence
  • If you miss one, move on immediately — don't fall behind
  • Write as you listen — don't wait until after the recording ends
  • Listen for signpost language: "Now I'd like to…", "Moving on to…", "Turning to…"
  • Listen for corrections: "Actually…", "Sorry, I meant…" — the corrected version is the answer

Spelling & Numbers

  • Names are often spelled out in the recording — write each letter as you hear it
  • Numbers: "fourteen" vs "forty", "sixty" vs "sixteen" — listen to the ending
  • Dates: "the fifth of March" → 5th March or 5/3 — both are acceptable
  • Transfer time: use the full 10 minutes to check spelling carefully, especially on proper nouns
⚠ -teen vs -ty: fourteen (14) sounds like "forTEEN"; forty (40) sounds like "FORty"

Practice Exercises

How to Use These Exercises These exercises simulate the listening task using printed dialogues. Read the transcript below as if you are listening — cover the questions first, read at a steady pace, and fill in answers as you go. Then check against the answer key.
Section 1 Simulation — Sports Centre Registration

Receptionist: Good morning, City Sports Centre. How can I help you?
Caller: Hi, I'd like to register for membership please.
Receptionist: Of course. Can I take your full name?
Caller: Yes, it's Sophie Hartmann. That's H-A-R-T-M-A-N-N.
Receptionist: Thank you. And your date of birth?
Caller: The fourteenth of March, 1992.
Receptionist: Postcode?
Caller: SW3 7BQ.
Receptionist: And which membership type would you like? We have Standard at £35 a month, or Premium at £55.
Caller: I'll take the Standard, please.
Receptionist: Great. Are there any specific facilities you're interested in?
Caller: Yes — mainly the swimming pool and yoga classes.
Receptionist: We run beginner yoga on Tuesdays at 6pm and intermediate on Thursdays at 7pm.
Caller: The Thursday class would suit me better.
Receptionist: Perfect. I'll send a confirmation to your email. Can you give me that?
Caller: It's s.hartmann at webmail dot co dot uk.
Receptionist: Wonderful. Your membership starts from Monday. You'll need to collect your card from the front desk between 9am and 5pm.
Caller: Thank you very much.

Section 1 — Complete the Registration Form (NO MORE than three words and/or a number)

  1. Surname: _______________
  2. Date of birth: _______________
  3. Postcode: _______________
  4. Membership type: _______________
  5. Cost per month: £_______________
  6. Preferred yoga class: _______________
  7. Email address: _______________
  8. Card collection hours: 9am–_______________
Show Answers
  1. Hartmann
  2. 14th March 1992 (or 14/3/1992)
  3. SW3 7BQ
  4. Standard
  5. 35
  6. Thursday (7pm) (or intermediate yoga / Thursdays)
  7. s.hartmann@webmail.co.uk
  8. 5pm
Section 3 Simulation — Students Discussing a Research Project

Tutor: So you've both chosen urban heat islands as your topic. What angle are you taking?
Student A (Maya): We're focusing on the social impact — specifically, which groups are most vulnerable to extreme heat events.
Student B (Luca): Our main finding is that elderly residents in low-income areas are disproportionately at risk, because they're less likely to have air conditioning and less able to leave the city during heatwaves.
Tutor: That's an important focus. What sources have you been using?
Maya: Mostly peer-reviewed journals, but we've also looked at WHO reports on climate health.
Tutor: Good. And what do you see as the main policy recommendation from your research?
Luca: We think the most effective measure would be mandatory green roofs on new buildings — they reduce surface temperatures significantly.
Maya: We also argue that cool spaces — libraries, community centres — should be opened as emergency shelters during extreme heat.
Tutor: Have you considered the cost angle? Some would argue green roofs are too expensive to mandate.
Luca: We address that — the long-term health savings actually outweigh the construction costs according to the studies we found.
Tutor: Very thorough. What's your biggest challenge right now?
Maya: Finding recent data for Central Asia, honestly. Most studies focus on Europe or North America.

Section 3 — Choose the correct answer A, B, or C

  1. The students' research focuses primarily on… A) The environmental causes of urban heat islands B) The social effects of extreme urban heat C) Economic solutions to the heat island problem
  2. According to the students, elderly low-income residents are particularly at risk because… A) They live in areas with more traffic pollution B) They lack access to cooling and cannot easily relocate C) They are less aware of the health risks of heat
  3. The students' primary policy recommendation is… A) Opening emergency cool spaces in community buildings B) Requiring green roofs on new construction C) Increasing public transport during heatwaves
  4. What challenge do the students mention? A) Limited access to academic journals B) Disagreement about the policy recommendations C) Lack of recent data for their region
Show Answers & Explanations
  1. B — "focusing on the social impact" is stated directly by Maya
  2. B — "less likely to have air conditioning and less able to leave the city" = lack access to cooling / cannot relocate
  3. B — Luca says "the most effective measure would be mandatory green roofs on new buildings" (A is mentioned but described as secondary)
  4. C — Maya says "Finding recent data for Central Asia" — paraphrased as "lack of recent data for their region"

Practice & Resources

Examiner Tips

  • Polish your topic vocabulary Section 3 is almost always about education. For example, students and a tutor talking about an assignment. Learn all the vocabulary you can about studying at university. This way you will understand this section better.
  • Word types Skip over the questions and decide which type of word fits in each gap. Is it a noun, verb, adverb or adjective? Write 'N' for noun, 'V' for verb and so on. This will help you to focus on the specific word forms while listening.
  • Watch out words-indicators Listen for words-indicators, such as however , but , then , finally . They help you to anticipate what the speaker will say.
  • Check for silly mistakes After each section you have 30 seconds to check your answers. It is important to check spelling, plurals and word forms. Remember that only correctly written answers will gain points.
  • Don't leave any blank answers! You won't lose marks for incorrect answers, so even if you don't know the answer it is better to write something in the answers box. Read the question again and make a guess!
