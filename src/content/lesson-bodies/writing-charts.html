Task 1 · Report

Charts, Graphs & Tables

What is it?

You are given a bar chart, line graph, pie chart or table (sometimes two combined) and must summarise the information in at least 150 words. You select and compare the key features — you do not list every number, and you never give an opinion.

First: The Method

Every chart report uses the same four paragraphs — introduction, overview, two detail paragraphs. That method is covered once, in How to Answer Task 1. This lesson is about the chart-specific part: what to look for in the data before you write.

Reading the Chart — What to Look For

First decide which of the two kinds of chart you have — it changes everything you write:

Change over time (line graph, dated bars)Static comparison (pie, table, one-date bars)
Key features to findThe overall direction · the highest peak and lowest point · the fastest change · any crossover where lines swap places · start vs end valuesThe largest and smallest categories · anything roughly equal · anything that dominates (more than half) · striking gaps between groups
Overview saysWhat rose, what fell, what changed mostWhat ranks first and last, and the biggest contrast
Group details byDirection: the risers together, the fallers togetherSize: the major categories, then the minor ones
TensePast period → past simple; projection → is expected to reachNo dates → present simple

Tables: scan both directions — down the columns for the biggest rows, across the rows for patterns — and report the extremes, not the middle.

Two visuals together (a common mixed question: bar chart + pie chart, line graph + table, two pie charts for two years): they may be the same kind twice, or two different kinds combined. Either way, your overview must connect them ("while X…, Y…") — never describe them one after the other as if they were separate reports. When the two are different types, read each with its own column above, then decide whether your two body paragraphs split by chart (everything from chart 1, then chart 2) or by theme (whichever links the two most naturally, e.g. one category discussed across both visuals).

Trend Language

DirectionVerbsNouns
Uprose, climbed, increased, surgeda rise, an increase, an upward trend
Downfell, dropped, declined, plummeteda fall, a drop, a decline
Flatremained stable, levelled off, plateaueda plateau, little change
Up & downfluctuated, varieda fluctuation, volatility
Extremespeaked at, hit a low ofa peak, a high point, a trough

Grade the change with adverbs: slightly, gradually, steadily, considerably, sharply, dramatically. For proportions: a quarter of, more than half, the vast majority, one in five.

Full Worked Examples

One complete question, model report and Band 8+ checklist per type — open whichever you want to study:

Line graph — full worked example (question, image & model report)
Question

The graph below shows the percentage of the workforce employed in three sectors of a country between 1980 and 2020. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.
Show model report (Band 8+ features)

The line graph illustrates changes in the proportion of the workforce employed in agriculture, manufacturing and services in a country between 1980 and 2020.

Overall, there was a dramatic shift away from agricultural employment and towards the services sector, which overtook both of the other sectors during the period and ended up dominating the workforce, while manufacturing's share declined only gradually by comparison.

Agriculture began the period as the largest employer, accounting for 45% of the workforce in 1980, but this figure fell steadily over the four decades to reach just 8% by 2020 — a drop of well over half. Manufacturing followed a much gentler downward path, easing from 25% to 18% over the same period, with the bulk of that decline concentrated in the final decade.

The services sector moved in the opposite direction, expanding consistently from 30% in 1980 to 74% by 2020, by far the largest share of any sector. It overtook manufacturing at around 1990, when both stood at roughly a third of the workforce, and went on to overtake agriculture too by the mid-2000s, eventually accounting for almost three times the combined share of the other two sectors.


  • Overview names the headline movement and the crossover, with no figures ✓
  • Crossovers called out explicitly with approximate timing ✓
  • The two declining sectors are grouped together; the rising sector gets its own paragraph ✓
  • Precise start/end figures paired with comparative language throughout ✓
Bar chart — full worked example (question, image & model report)
Question

The chart below shows the percentage of households with internet access in five countries in 2010 and 2020. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.
Show model report (Band 8+ features)

The bar chart compares the proportion of homes with an internet connection across five nations in 2010 and 2020.

Overall, connectivity rose in every country over the decade, but the scale of the change differed enormously: the countries that started lowest grew fastest, considerably narrowing the gap with the early adopters.

In 2010, the two wealthiest countries already enjoyed widespread access, at 81% and 76% respectively, and these figures edged up to around 95% by 2020. Growth here was steady but modest, since most households were already online at the start of the period.

The remaining three countries present a strikingly different picture. Access in the lowest-ranked nation more than tripled, jumping from just 18% to 61%, while the other two roughly doubled their figures to reach 70% and 74%. As a result, by 2020 no country in the group had less than three-fifths of its households connected, compared with fewer than one in five a decade earlier.


  • Introduction paraphrases (shows → compares; percentage of households → proportion of homes) ✓
  • Overview states the two big trends, no figures ✓
  • Bodies group the data logically (leaders vs catchers-up), not country by country ✓
  • Selective figures with comparative language throughout ✓
Pie chart — full worked example (question, image & model report)
Question

The pie charts below show the sources of electricity generation in a country in 2000 and in 2020. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.
Show model report (Band 8+ features)

The two pie charts compare the breakdown of electricity generation by source in a single country in 2000 and 2020.

Overall, the country's electricity mix shifted away from fossil fuels and towards renewable energy over the two decades: coal, which supplied more than half of all electricity in 2000, was overtaken by renewables as the leading source by 2020, while gas held a relatively stable share throughout.

In 2000, coal was overwhelmingly dominant, generating 55% of the country's electricity — more than the other four sources combined. Gas supplied a further quarter of output (25%), while nuclear power and renewables contributed comparatively modest shares of 12% and 5% respectively, with other sources making up the remaining 3%.

By 2020, this picture had reversed dramatically. Renewables became the single largest source, rising eightfold to 40% of generation, while coal's share collapsed to just 15%, less than a third of its 2000 level. Gas increased slightly to 30%, overtaking coal to become the second-largest source, whereas nuclear declined marginally to 10% and other sources edged up to 5%.


  • Overview states the headline shift (coal overtaken by renewables) without repeating every figure ✓
  • Each year gets its own paragraph, but 2020 is explicitly compared back to 2000 ✓
  • Dominant and emerging categories get more attention than minor ones ✓
  • Multiplier language (eightfold, less than a third) used alongside percentages ✓

Question

The table below shows the average number of books read per person per year in four countries in 2000, 2010 and 2020. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.
Country200020102020
Finland121415
Japan10911
USA876
Brazil346
Show model report (Band 8+ features)

The table shows how many books, on average, people read per year in four different countries in 2000, 2010 and 2020.

Overall, reading habits diverged sharply across the four countries: Finland consistently read the most and increased its figure further over the period, the USA was the only country to see a sustained decline, while Japan and Brazil both experienced a dip followed by a recovery.

Finland recorded the highest figures throughout, rising from an already substantial 12 books per year in 2000 to 15 in 2020, a gain of three books. The USA moved in the opposite direction, falling from 8 books in 2000 to a low of 6 in 2020, having dipped to 7 in the intervening decade — the only steady downward trend among the four countries.

Japan and Brazil both followed a dip-then-recovery pattern, though from very different starting points. Japan's figure slipped slightly from 10 to 9 books between 2000 and 2010 before rebounding to 11, its highest point over the period. Brazil, which read far fewer books than the other three countries throughout, doubled its figure from 3 to 6 over the two decades despite a small dip to 4 in 2010, meaning it saw the largest proportional increase of any country in the table.


  • Overview compares all four countries by direction of change, not a country-by-country list ✓
  • Figures are grouped by pattern (rising, falling, dip-then-recover), not read row by row ✓
  • Both absolute figures and proportional language used (doubled, largest proportional increase) ✓
  • Extremes reported — highest throughout, only steady decline — per the "extremes, not the middle" rule above ✓

Common Mistakes

⚠ Copying the question wording into the introduction ⚠ No overview — an instant Task Achievement ceiling of Band 5 ⚠ Listing every data point instead of selecting key features ⚠ Writing a conclusion or giving an opinion ⚠ Describing categories one by one with no comparison
