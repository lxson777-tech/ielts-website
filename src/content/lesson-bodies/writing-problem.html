Problem & Solution Essays

What is it?

The prompt names a situation and asks some combination of causes, problems, effects and solutions. Four different question patterns hide under this one umbrella, so identify yours before you plan, because the wrong pattern changes what your two body paragraphs must contain. (The four-paragraph method itself is covered in How to Answer Task 2. This lesson is about adapting it to these questions.)

The Matching Principle

  • Every solution should answer a problem you raised: a solution to an unmentioned problem earns nothing
  • Two items per paragraph, well developed, beat five listed thinly
  • Say who acts: governments, employers, schools, individuals. Solutions without an actor sound empty
  • Evaluate briefly: is the solution realistic? has it worked somewhere?

The Cause + Effect pattern is the trap: writers who are used to "problem + solution" often add solutions nobody asked for. If the question never says "solution", "solve", "measure" or "address", don't propose any. You'll be answering a question that wasn't asked, which caps Task Response regardless of how well the paragraph is written.

Structure (4 paragraphs)

  1. Introduction: paraphrase the situation + roadmap matching your pattern: "This essay examines the main difficulties this creates and proposes two measures to address them" (Problem+Solution) or "This essay will examine why this is happening and the effects it is having" (Cause+Effect).
  2. Body 1: the first half of your pattern (causes / problems), each explained with a consequence or example.
  3. Body 2: the second half of your pattern (solutions / effects). Matched one-for-one where the pattern requires matching.
  4. Conclusion: one sentence of summary + an outlook ("Unless cities act, congestion will only worsen.")

Example question

Traffic congestion in large cities is getting worse every year. What problems does this cause, and what measures could governments take to solve them?
Show model body paragraphs (condensed)

The most serious consequence of congestion is economic: commuters lose working hours in traffic, and goods become more expensive to move. A second, less visible cost is public health, since idling engines concentrate pollution exactly where people live and work, giving rise to respiratory illness.

Both problems can be tackled if governments make private car use less attractive than the alternatives. Congestion charging, already successful in London and Singapore, directly reduces the number of vehicles and funds public transport. The health burden, meanwhile, would be eased by investing those revenues in electric bus fleets and protected cycle lanes…


  • Two problems, each with a developed consequence ✓
  • Solutions match the problems one-for-one, with a named actor ✓
  • Evaluation grounded in real examples (London, Singapore) ✓

Common Mistakes

⚠ Adding solutions to a Cause + Effect question that never asked for any ⚠ Answering "causes" when the question asked "problems" (or vice versa) ⚠ Solutions that don't match any stated problem or cause ⚠ A shopping list of five one-line ideas instead of two developed ones ⚠ "People should be more aware". A slogan, not a solution
