About the Test

IELTS Writing at a Glance

The Basics

  • Total time: 60 minutes for two tasks — you manage the split yourself
  • Recommended: 20 minutes on Task 1, 40 minutes on Task 2
  • Minimum lengths: 150 words (Task 1) and 250 words (Task 2) — under-length answers are penalised
  • Task 2 is worth twice as much as Task 1 — never let it run out of time
  • No choice of question — you must answer what you are given
  • Notes and bullet points are not assessed — answers must be fully written out

The Two Tasks

  • Task 1 (Academic): a report describing a chart, graph, table, map, process or diagram. Facts only — no opinion, no conclusion.
  • Task 1 (General Training): a letter — formal, semi-formal or informal — covering all three bullet points in the prompt.
  • Task 2 (both modules): a formal essay responding to an opinion, discussion, problem/solution or two-part question.
  • Task 2 topics are general interest — no specialist knowledge needed; you are marked on your English, not your ideas.

How Examiners Mark It

Each task is scored on four criteria, weighted equally (25% each). The examiner awards a whole band (0–9) per criterion, and the task score is their average.

CriterionWhat it measures
Task Achievement / Task ResponseDid you fully answer the task — all parts, right format, developed ideas (and an overview in a Task 1 report)?
Coherence & CohesionLogical paragraphing, clear progression, and natural linking — not mechanical connectors.
Lexical ResourceRange and precision of vocabulary, collocation, and spelling.
Grammatical Range & AccuracyVariety of sentence structures and how error-free they are.

Your overall Writing band = (Task 1 + Task 2 × 2) ÷ 3, rounded to the nearest half band. Our Essay Checker scores you on exactly these criteria, using the official public band descriptors.

What this means for your target Because Task 2 counts double, a Band 7 essay with a Band 6 report already averages 6.5–7. Master the essay first, then polish the report. And watch the clock: the single most common writing disaster is a brilliant Task 1 followed by a rushed, unfinished Task 2.
