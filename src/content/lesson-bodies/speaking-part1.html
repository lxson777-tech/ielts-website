1
Test Format

What is Speaking Part 1?

The Basics

  • Face-to-face with an examiner (or via video call)
  • Lasts 4-5 minutes
  • Around 12 questions across 3 topics
  • All questions are about you, your life, and your country
  • Topics are everyday: home, hobbies, food, weather, transport…
  • The test is recorded for possible re-marking

The Full Test at a Glance

  • Part 1: 4-5 min / Q&A / personal topics (today)
  • Part 2: 3-4 min / individual talk / cue card
  • Part 3: 4-5 min / discussion / world issues

Total test: 11-14 minutes. All three parts use the same marking criteria.

What Kind of Questions to Expect

Part 1 questions are not difficult: they are designed to be easy so you can warm up and speak naturally. The examiner is not testing your knowledge; they are giving you an opportunity to show your English. You will typically get 4 questions on each of 3 topics.

✓ Do you enjoy cooking? ✓ What kind of music do you listen to? ✓ How often do you use public transport? ✓ Did you have a pet as a child?
Important: This is an informal test Informal language is completely normal and acceptable in Part 1. You do not need to sound formal or academic. Speak naturally, as you would in a friendly conversation.
2
Marking Criteria

How You Are Scored

Your score is NOT given after each part. The examiner marks your performance across the whole test. There are four criteria, each worth 25%.

25%
Fluency & Coherence

Speak at a natural pace without long pauses. Ideas should connect logically.

25%
Lexical Resource

Use a wide and varied vocabulary. Avoid repeating the same basic words.

25%
Grammatical Range & Accuracy

Use a mix of simple and complex sentence structures with few errors.

25%
Pronunciation

Be clear and easy to understand. Accent is fine. Clarity is what matters.

What This Means for Part 1

  • Fluency: Don't rush to fill silence with "um, um, um". Use natural fillers like "Well…", "Actually…", "That's a good question…"
  • Vocabulary: Say "enjoyable", "fascinating", "rewarding" instead of "good". Say "enormous", "spacious", "considerable" instead of "big".
  • Grammar: Mix tenses. Use conditionals, comparatives, and relative clauses where natural.
  • Pronunciation: Speak at a steady pace, stress the right syllables, and link words naturally.
⚠ Accent does NOT affect your score. Clarity does ⚠ You are marked on the whole test, not part by part
3
Strategy

How to Approach Part 1

How Long Should Your Answer Be?

Part 1 answers should be 2-4 sentences. Not one word. Not a paragraph. Give a direct answer, then add one reason, example, or detail to extend it.

Think: Answer → Extend → (Optional detail)

Should I Speak Fast or Slow?

Speak at a natural, comfortable pace: not fast to impress, not artificially slow. Speed does not equal fluency. Fluency means speaking smoothly with ideas connected naturally.

Key Strategy Points

  1. Prepare topics in advance: topics repeat every year. Know your opinions on home, food, hobbies, transport, weather, music, etc.
  2. Don't memorise full answers: examiners can spot memorised responses. Prepare ideas and vocabulary, then speak naturally.
  3. Extend every answer: the biggest mistake is giving one-word or one-sentence answers. Always add a reason or example.
  4. Showcase vocabulary naturally: don't force in difficult words. Use richer vocabulary where it fits naturally.
  5. You can ask for clarification: "Sorry, could you repeat that?" or "Do you mean…?" is perfectly fine.
  6. You can self-correct: if you make a mistake and notice it, correct yourself briefly and move on.
  7. You can invent details: the examiner is testing your English, not your honesty.
⚠ Don't give one-word answers ⚠ Don't memorise whole model answers ✓ Prepare vocabulary, not scripts ✓ Always extend your answer

Useful Fillers & Linking Phrases

These help you sound fluent and give you a moment to think naturally:

Well, actually… To be honest… I'd say that… That's a good question… As far as I know… What I mean is… Having said that… I suppose… Funnily enough…
4
Talk Structure

How to Structure a Part 1 Answer

Part 1 answers are short. 2-4 sentences, not a mini-speech. Use the A.R.E. method so every answer feels complete without dragging on.

A
Answer. Respond Directly (1 sentence)

Answer the question head-on in your first sentence. Yes, no, sometimes, rarely. Don't dodge it or ramble before getting to the point.

↓ conjunction: given that… / seeing as… / since…
R
Reason. Say Why (1 sentence)

Give the reason behind your answer. Instead of a plain "because", link it with something more advanced, such as given that…, seeing as…, since…, a small swap that shows off grammatical range.

↓ conjunction: even though… / whereas… / not only… but also…
E
Extend. Add a Detail or Example (1 sentence)

Add a specific example, memory, or a contrasting detail using even though…, whereas…, or not only… but also…: these connect two ideas in one breath instead of two flat sentences.

A.R.E. in Action

Question: Do you enjoy cooking?

"Yes, I actually really enjoy it, given that it lets me switch off completely after a long day at work. Even though I don't always have much free time, I've recently started experimenting with Korean recipes, which has been a fun challenge."

  • A. Answer: "Yes, I actually really enjoy it"
  • R. Reason: "given that it lets me switch off completely after a long day at work"
  • E. Extend: "Even though I don't always have much free time, I've recently started experimenting with Korean recipes, which has been a fun challenge"
✓ Two advanced conjunctions in one answer is plenty. Don't overload every sentence ⚠ Skipping the Reason is the most common cause of answers that feel too short
5
Answer Technique

Weak vs. Strong Answers

Study the difference between a weak answer and a strong one. The topic is the same. Only the technique changes.

Question: Do you enjoy cooking?
✗ Weak Answer (Band 4-5)
"Yes, I like cooking. I cook every day."

Too short. No reason, no detail, no interesting vocabulary.

✓ Strong Answer (Band 7+)
"Yes, I actually find cooking quite therapeutic. After a long day at work, I enjoy experimenting with different recipes. Lately I've been trying to cook more Korean dishes, which has been a real challenge but also really rewarding."
  • A. Answer: "Yes, I actually find cooking quite therapeutic."
  • R. Reason: "After a long day at work, I enjoy experimenting with different recipes"
  • E. Extend: "lately I've been trying to cook more Korean dishes, which has been a real challenge but also really rewarding."

Good vocabulary (therapeutic, experimenting, rewarding), specific detail, sounds natural.

Question: What kind of music do you listen to?
✗ Weak Answer
"I listen to pop music. I like it."

Extremely basic vocabulary, no extension, no personal touch.

✓ Strong Answer
"Well, my taste is quite varied to be honest. I mostly gravitate towards jazz and indie music. There's something about the laid-back rhythms that I find really relaxing. I also grew up listening to a lot of classical music, so that still has a special place for me."
  • A. Answer: "Well, my taste is quite varied to be honest. I mostly gravitate towards jazz and indie music"
  • R. Reason: "there's something about the laid-back rhythms that I find really relaxing"
  • E. Extend: "I also grew up listening to a lot of classical music, so that still has a special place for me."

Rich vocabulary (gravitate, laid-back, varied), uses past tense naturally, sounds conversational.

Question: How do you usually get to work or school?
✗ Weak Answer
"I take the bus. It takes 20 minutes."

Just facts, no opinion, no vocabulary range.

✓ Strong Answer
"I usually take public transport. The metro, specifically. It's fairly convenient where I live, and it saves me from dealing with traffic, which can be absolutely chaotic during rush hour. I'd say it takes around 30 minutes door-to-door, which I actually use to read or listen to podcasts."
  • A. Answer: "I usually take public transport, the metro, specifically."
  • R. Reason: "It's fairly convenient where I live, and it saves me from dealing with traffic, which can be absolutely chaotic during rush hour."
  • E. Extend: "I'd say it takes around 30 minutes door-to-door, which I actually use to read or listen to podcasts."

Specific vocabulary (chaotic, convenient, door-to-door), gives reasons and habits.

Every strong answer above follows the A.R.E. structure from the Talk Structure section. Answer, reason, then a detail or example.

6
Practice

Practice Topics & Questions

How to Use This Section For each topic: read the questions, then answer them out loud (or in writing). Aim for 2-4 sentences per answer using the Answer → Reason → Detail formula.

Work

  1. What is your job?
  2. Why did you choose that job?
  3. Do you like your job?
  4. Do you get on well with your colleagues?
  5. Would you change your job if you could?
  6. Do you plan to continue with this job in the future?

Study

  1. What do you study?
  2. Why did you choose that subject?
  3. Do you enjoy it?
  4. What are the main aspects of your subject?
  5. Would you change your subject if you could?
  6. Do you plan to work in the same field?

Home

  1. Do you live in a house or a flat?
  2. Who do you live with?
  3. What is your favourite room and why?
  4. What would you change about your home?
  5. Do you plan to live there in the future?
  6. What is your neighbourhood like?

Hometown

  1. Where is your hometown?
  2. Do you like your hometown?
  3. What is your hometown like?
  4. How could your hometown be improved?
  5. Has it changed much since you were a child?
  6. Is it a good place to bring up children?

Music

  1. What kind of music do you enjoy?
  2. Did you listen to music as a child?
  3. Do you play a musical instrument?
  4. Is music important in your culture?

Food

  1. Do you enjoy cooking?
  2. What is your favourite food?
  3. Is there any food you dislike?
  4. How important is food in your culture?

Transport

  1. How do you usually travel around your city?
  2. Do you prefer public or private transport?
  3. Is public transport good in your country?
  4. Has transport changed much in your lifetime?

Childhood

  1. Did you enjoy your childhood?
  2. What is your earliest memory?
  3. What did you enjoy doing as a child?
  4. Did you have a lot of friends growing up?

Self-Assessment. After Each Answer, Check:

  1. Did I answer the question directly?
  2. Did I give at least one reason or explanation?
  3. Did I add a personal detail, memory, or example?
  4. Did I use at least one piece of vocabulary beyond basic words?
  5. Was my answer 2-4 sentences long?
  6. Did I use a natural filler or linking phrase at least once?
Show Sample Answer. "Do you enjoy cooking?" (Band 7+)

"Well, to be honest, I wouldn't say I'm a passionate cook, but I do enjoy it when I have the time. I find there's something quite satisfying about preparing a meal from scratch. Especially when it turns out well! Lately I've been trying to learn a few traditional dishes from my country, which has been both challenging and fun."


  • Answer: "I do enjoy it when I have the time"
  • Reason: "something quite satisfying about preparing a meal from scratch"
  • Detail: "trying to learn traditional dishes… challenging and fun"
  • Good vocab: satisfying, from scratch, challenging
  • Natural filler: "Well, to be honest…"
Show Sample Answer. "What is your hometown like?" (Band 7+)

"I'm from Almaty, which is the largest city in Kazakhstan. It's a really vibrant and modern city, surrounded by the Tian Shan mountains, which gives it a spectacular backdrop. I'd say it has a nice mix of old Soviet architecture and very contemporary buildings. It's changed enormously in the last twenty years or so."


  • Answer: Describes the city directly
  • Detail: Mountains, architecture, change over time
  • Good vocab: vibrant, spectacular backdrop, contemporary, enormously
  • Natural filler: "I'd say…"
