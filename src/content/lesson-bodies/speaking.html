About the Test

IELTS Speaking at a Glance

The Basics

  • A face-to-face interview with a real examiner, lasting 11–14 minutes
  • Three parts, always in the same order — interview, cue-card talk, discussion
  • The same test for Academic and General Training
  • The interview is recorded, so a remark can be re-marked if you appeal
  • Often taken on a different day from the written papers
  • There are no right answers — you are marked on your English, not your opinions

The Three Parts

  • Part 1 (4–5 min): questions about familiar topics — home, work, studies, hobbies. Warm-up pace, short-but-developed answers.
  • Part 2 (3–4 min): a cue card with a topic and prompts. 1 minute to prepare, then speak for up to 2 minutes alone.
  • Part 3 (4–5 min): a two-way discussion of abstract questions growing out of the Part 2 topic — the place to show complex language.

How Examiners Mark It

Four criteria, weighted equally (25% each). The examiner scores each as a whole band and averages them.

CriterionWhat it measures
Fluency & CoherenceSpeaking at length without unnatural pauses, self-correction or lost threads — and answers that hang together logically.
Lexical ResourceRange and precision of vocabulary, idiomatic language, and the ability to paraphrase when a word won't come.
Grammatical Range & AccuracyVariety of structures — conditionals, relative clauses, comparatives — and how error-free they are.
PronunciationBeing effortlessly understood: individual sounds, word stress, sentence rhythm and intonation. Accent is not penalised.

Score → Band Conversion

Your four criterion scores are averaged, then rounded using IELTS’s official rule: an average ending in .25 rounds up to the next half band, and an average ending in .75 rounds up to the next whole band — there is no rounding down. The same rule combines Listening, Reading, Writing and Speaking into your Overall Band.

Average of 4 criteriaReported band
x.00x.0 (no change)
x.25x.5 (rounds up)
x.50x.5 (no change)
x.75(x+1).0 (rounds up)

Example: 7, 6, 6, 6 averages to 6.25 → rounds up to Band 6.5. 7, 7, 7, 6 averages to 6.75 → rounds up to Band 7.0.

What this means for your target Fluency counts as much as grammar — a hesitant answer full of perfect tenses scores lower than a flowing one with small slips. Practise speaking in full answers, out loud, against the clock. Every lesson below ends with practice material for exactly that.
