Task 2 · The Universal Method

One Approach for Every Task 2

What Task 2 Really Asks

  • A formal essay responding to a question — the same task for Academic and General Training
  • 40 minutes, at least 250 words (aim for 270–320)
  • Worth twice as much as Task 1 — never let it run short on time
  • You are marked on your English, not your ideas — no specialist knowledge is needed
  • A clear, consistent position is compulsory in every essay type, even the ones that ask you to discuss both sides

The Same Method Every Time

Whether the question asks you to agree or disagree, discuss both views, analyse a problem, or answer two direct questions, the essay has the same four-paragraph skeleton. Learn the method once, then each type lesson tells you how to adapt it to that specific question.

What changes between types is what goes inside the two body paragraphs — not whether you have an introduction and conclusion.

The Four Paragraphs

ParagraphJobHow
1 · Introduction
(2 sentences)		 Paraphrase the question, then answer it Sentence 1: reword the topic — change the vocabulary and sentence structure, never copy it. Sentence 2: state your position, or preview both views + your opinion for a discussion essay.
2 · Body 1 Your first main point, fully developed Use TEE: Topic sentence → Explanation (why is this true?) → Example (a concrete illustration). One clear idea, not three half-formed ones.
3 · Body 2 Your second main point, or the counter-view Same TEE shape. For opinion essays this can be a second reason, or the concession ("Admittedly…, however…"); for discussion essays, this is where the opposing view gets its fair explanation.
4 · Conclusion
(1–2 sentences)		 Restate your position in fresh words Summarise, don't repeat word-for-word. No new ideas or examples — anything new here looks like an afterthought, not a conclusion.

The 40-Minute Plan

  1. Minutes 1–5 — analyse and plan. What type of question is this — opinion, discussion, problem/solution, two-part? What is being asked, exactly? Choose your position and your two supporting ideas before writing a word — a plan on paper prevents you wandering off-topic halfway through.
  2. Minutes 6–35 — write the four paragraphs. Roughly 270–320 words is the sweet spot: enough to develop ideas fully, not so much that quality drops from rushing.
  3. Minutes 36–40 — check. Word count over 250? Position clear and unchanged from introduction to conclusion? Every part of the question answered? Any repeated basic words to upgrade?

How the Four Criteria Apply to Task 2

Criterion (25% each)What it means here
Task ResponseEvery part of the question answered, a clear and consistent position, ideas developed with explanation and example (not just listed), 250+ words
Coherence & CohesionThe four-paragraph shape, one central idea per paragraph, natural linking (however, as a result, in addition) — not a mechanical connector at the start of every sentence
Lexical ResourceRange and precision of vocabulary for the topic, accurate collocation, avoiding repetition of basic words like good/bad/very/thing
Grammatical Range & AccuracyA mix of simple and complex sentences (conditionals, relative clauses), used correctly more often than not
Golden rules Never leave the position ambiguous. Never introduce a new idea in the conclusion. Never pad with irrelevant general knowledge. Never write a memorised paragraph — examiners are trained to spot them, and it is heavily penalised. Never let Task 2 run under 40 minutes of your time.
✓ One well-developed idea beats three underdeveloped ones ✓ Read the question twice — answering the wrong question caps Task Response low, however well it's written ⚠ Under 250 words = automatic Task Response penalty
