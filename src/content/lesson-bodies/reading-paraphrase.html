Paraphrase Spotting

What is it?

Every IELTS Reading question — whatever its type — tests the same underlying skill: can you tell that a question is describing the same idea as a sentence in the passage, even though almost none of the words match? The passage almost never repeats the question's exact wording. If it did, Reading would just be a word-search.

Why it matters

Weak readers hunt for matching words and get pulled toward the wrong sentence, or toward a distractor built from the right words in the wrong combination. Strong readers hunt for matching meaning — a paraphrase can change every word and still be correct, or keep half the words and still be a trap.

Common Ways the Passage Paraphrases a Question

  • Synonym swap — "rose sharply" ⇄ "increased rapidly".
  • Word-class change — "the population increased" ⇄ "an increase in population".
  • Cause/effect reordering — "X led to Y" ⇄ "Y happened because of X" (watch for distractors that reverse which one caused which).
  • Quantifier shift — "most", "a few", "none", "all" carry very different meanings — a distractor often swaps one for another.
  • Hedged vs absolute language — "may reduce symptoms in some patients" is not the same claim as "cures the illness".
  • Idiom → literal meaning — "caught off guard" ⇄ "unprepared"; "a roaring trade" ⇄ "a successful business".
  • Numbers restated differently — "fewer than one in ten" ⇄ "the vast majority do not…".
⚠ Trap: a distractor reuses passage words but reverses the meaning (cause↔effect, more↔less) ⚠ Trap: an option adds a claim the passage never actually makes ⚠ Trap: hedged language ("may", "some", "believed") turned into an absolute claim

How to Practise It

  1. Read the option and the "passage" sentence below it separately, then ask: do they make the same claim, or just share some words?
  2. Underline the words that carry the meaning (verbs, quantifiers, negatives) — not just topic nouns.
  3. Check direction: who is doing what to whom? A reversed cause/effect is the single most common trap.
  4. Check strength: is the passage certain, or hedged with "may"/"some"/"believed"? Match that strength, don't upgrade it.
