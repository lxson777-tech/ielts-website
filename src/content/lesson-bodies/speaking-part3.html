Test Format

What is Speaking Part 3?

The Basics

  • Lasts 4-5 minutes
  • Examiner asks abstract, discussion questions linked to your Part 2 topic
  • Questions are about society, trends, opinions, and world issues
  • There is a two-way conversation: the examiner may agree, disagree, or probe further
  • This is the most intellectually demanding part of the test

Part 3 vs. Part 1

Part 1Part 3
Personal topicsSocial/global topics
"Do you enjoy cooking?""Why do some people prefer eating out?"
Simple opinionsReasoned arguments
Short answers (2-4 sentences)Extended answers (4-6+ sentences)
Casual, informalMore formal vocabulary appropriate
There are no right or wrong answers in Part 3 The examiner is not judging your opinions. Only your English. It is perfectly fine to disagree with a conventional view, take an unusual position, or change your mind mid-answer. What matters is how clearly and fluently you can express and justify ideas.
2
Question Types

5 Types of Part 3 Questions

1. Opinion Questions

"Do you think social media has a positive or negative effect on society?"

State your view, give reasons, and acknowledge the other side. Avoid one-word answers.

2. Compare / Contrast Questions

"How do attitudes towards the environment differ between older and younger generations?"

Identify key differences and explain why they exist. Use comparative language.

3. Cause / Effect Questions

"What are the main reasons why so many people are moving to cities?"

Identify 2-3 causes or effects and explain each one briefly. Don't just list them.

4. Solutions / Suggestions Questions

"What could governments do to encourage people to use public transport?"

Propose 2-3 practical ideas and explain why they would work. Use conditionals.

5. Speculative / Hypothetical Questions

"How do you think technology will change the way we work in the future?"

Use future forms and tentative language. No one expects certainty. The question asks you to speculate. Use phrases like "I imagine…", "It seems likely that…", "There's a strong possibility that…"

Key Language

Essential Part 3 Phrases

Expressing Strong Opinions

  • I firmly believe that…
  • In my view / In my opinion…
  • I'm convinced that…
  • It seems clear to me that…
  • I have no doubt that…
  • The way I see it…

Expressing Tentative Opinions

  • I tend to think that…
  • I'd say that, on balance…
  • It's hard to say, but I suppose…
  • I'm not entirely sure, but…
  • It depends, really…
  • To some extent, I believe…

Speculating About the Future

  • I imagine that… / I would imagine…
  • It seems likely that…
  • There's a real possibility that…
  • I wouldn't be surprised if…
  • It's hard to predict, but…
  • I suspect that in the long run…

Conceding / Balancing

  • Having said that…
  • That said, I do think…
  • On the other hand…
  • While I accept that…, I still believe…
  • Admittedly…
  • There's another side to this, of course…

Comparing

  • In contrast to…
  • Compared to older generations…
  • Whereas… / While…
  • Unlike… , …tends to…
  • The main difference is…
  • There's a marked distinction between…

Generalising

  • Generally speaking…
  • On the whole…
  • By and large…
  • As a rule…
  • In many cases…
  • Broadly speaking…
4
Extending Answers

How to Extend Your Answers: OREO

In Part 3, a short answer is not enough. Use the OREO formula to build extended, well-structured responses.

O
Opinion. State your position clearly

"I think / I believe / In my opinion…"

R
Reason. Explain why you hold this view

"The main reason is… / This is because… / Mainly because…"

E
Example. Give a specific illustration

"For example… / For instance… / Take [country/person/situation] as an example…"

O
Opinion (restate). Summarise or acknowledge the other side

"So on balance… / That's why I feel… / Having said that, I recognise that…"

Question: Do you think young people today are less physically active than previous generations?
✗ Weak (Band 5)
"Yes, I think so. Young people use phones a lot now and don't do sport. It's a problem."

No reasoning, no examples, too short, basic vocabulary.

✓ Strong (Band 7+)
"I tend to think that, on the whole, yes, today's youth are less physically active than previous generations. The main reason is the rise of sedentary entertainment, such as smartphones, streaming and gaming, which has replaced outdoor activities that used to be the default way of spending free time. Take South Korea, for example, where teenage gaming addiction has become a recognised public health concern. That said, I do think it's a more complex picture: participation in gym culture and organised sport has actually increased significantly among young adults in many countries. So while the trend exists, it's not universal."

OREO structure, good vocab, specific example, balanced view.

5
Practice

Practice Questions & Model Answers

How to Practise Read each question, think for 10 seconds, then answer out loud for 30-60 seconds. Try to use OREO structure and include phrases from Section 3. Then check the model answer.
Question: Why do some people prefer to travel abroad rather than explore their own country?
Show Model Answer

"I think there are several reasons for this. Firstly, there's often a novelty factor: people are drawn to the unfamiliar, whether that's a different language, cuisine, or landscape. For many, travelling abroad also carries a certain social status, since it's something to talk about, photograph, and share. Additionally, in an era of low-cost flights, international travel has become far more accessible than it used to be.

That said, I think there's also a tendency to undervalue what's nearby. I suspect many people would be surprised by how much their own country has to offer if they explored it with the same curiosity they bring to foreign destinations."

Question: How might the rise of artificial intelligence affect employment in the future?
Show Model Answer

"This is an area where I think the picture is genuinely mixed. On one hand, there's no doubt that AI will displace a significant number of jobs. Particularly in manufacturing, data processing, and routine administrative work. There's a strong possibility that entire categories of employment will simply disappear within a generation.

However, I wouldn't be surprised if new types of jobs emerge that we can't yet imagine. Just as the internet created industries that didn't exist in 1990. The more pressing concern, I think, is the transition period: making sure that workers whose jobs are automated have access to retraining, and that the benefits of AI productivity are distributed fairly rather than concentrating among a small technological elite."

Question: To what extent do you think governments should fund the arts?
Show Model Answer

"I firmly believe that governments have a responsibility to support the arts, though I'd argue the extent of that support needs to be balanced carefully. The core argument for public funding is that the arts provide a cultural identity and social commentary that the market alone won't sustain. If left entirely to commercial forces, only profitable, mainstream art forms would survive.

Take theatre, for instance: without state funding, small companies producing experimental or classical work simply couldn't exist. Admittedly, some would argue that taxpayers' money is better spent on healthcare or education. While I accept that these must take priority, I'd still maintain that a small allocation for the arts is money well spent, both economically (through cultural tourism) and socially, in terms of community wellbeing and national identity."
