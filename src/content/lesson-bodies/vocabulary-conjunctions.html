Conjunctions & Linking Words

Linking words fall into a handful of functions. Learn them by what they do, not as a random list, and you will always know which one a sentence needs.

Contrast

Instead of a plain "but", try although, whereas, despite or nevertheless.

Word / PhraseMeaningExample in Context
althoughintroduces a contrast within a single sentence, at the start or middle of a clause"Although public transport has improved, many commuters still prefer to drive."
whereaslinks two clauses that contrast directly with each other"City dwellers rely on public transport, whereas rural residents depend on private cars."
despite / in spite ofintroduces a contrasting fact, followed by a noun or gerund rather than a full clause"Despite the rising cost of living, consumer spending has continued to grow."
neverthelessintroduces a point that contrasts with what was just said, often to concede a counter-argument"The policy is expensive to implement; nevertheless, its long-term benefits are considerable."

Addition

Instead of a plain "and" or "also", try furthermore, moreover or in addition.

Word / PhraseMeaningExample in Context
furthermoreadds a further, often stronger, point to support the same argument"The scheme reduces congestion. Furthermore, it lowers emissions in the city centre."
moreoveradds an additional point, similar to furthermore, usually in formal writing"Remote working boosts productivity; moreover, it improves employees' work-life balance."
in additionadds an extra point to what has just been said, often starting a new sentence"In addition, the scheme would create thousands of new jobs in construction."

Result

Instead of a plain "so", try therefore, consequently or as a result.

Word / PhraseMeaningExample in Context
thereforeintroduces a logical result or conclusion drawn from the previous point"Fossil fuel reserves are finite; therefore, governments must invest in renewable alternatives."
consequentlyintroduces a direct consequence or effect of what was just stated"University fees have risen sharply; consequently, fewer students from low-income families are applying."
as a resultintroduces the outcome of something previously stated, similar to consequently"Fewer people smoke in public places; as a result, passive smoking rates have fallen sharply."

Condition

Instead of a plain "if", try provided that, unless or as long as.

Word / PhraseMeaningExample in Context
provided thatintroduces a necessary condition for something to happen"Nuclear power can be safe, provided that strict regulations are enforced."
unlessintroduces a condition meaning "if not""Traffic congestion will keep worsening unless cities invest in public transport."
as long asintroduces a condition, similar in meaning to provided that"Nuclear energy can be part of the solution, as long as waste is disposed of safely."

Purpose

Instead of a plain "to", try so that or in order to.

Word / PhraseMeaningExample in Context
so thatintroduces the purpose or intended result of an action"Many schools now teach digital literacy so that students can use technology responsibly."
in order tointroduces the purpose of an action, followed by a verb in the infinitive"Many cities have introduced congestion charges in order to reduce traffic in the centre."

Cause

Instead of a plain "because", try due to, owing to or since.

Word / PhraseMeaningExample in Context
due tointroduces the cause of something, followed by a noun phrase"Crop yields have fallen due to prolonged drought in the region."
owing tointroduces the cause of something, similar to due to, typically at the start of a sentence"Owing to advances in medicine, life expectancy has risen dramatically over the past century."
sinceintroduces a reason, often at the start of a sentence"Since resources are limited, recycling has become an economic necessity rather than a choice."

Useful Essay Phrases

  • "Although X has clear advantages, it also carries significant risks."
  • "Whereas some argue X, others maintain that Y is preferable."
  • "This trend can be explained by several factors. Furthermore, it is likely to continue."
  • "Governments must act quickly; otherwise, the problem will only worsen."
  • "Provided that adequate safeguards are in place, the benefits outweigh the drawbacks."
Why this matters for your band score Coherence and Cohesion is a full 25% of your Writing band. Varying your linking words, instead of repeating "and", "but" and "so" in every sentence, is one of the fastest ways to move from Band 6 to Band 7+.

Fill in the gaps using words from this section:

  1. ________ renewable energy is more expensive to install, it is far cheaper to run over time. (Although)
  2. Air pollution levels have fallen sharply; ________, respiratory illness rates have also declined. (consequently)
  3. Some countries rely heavily on tourism, ________ others depend mainly on manufacturing. (whereas)
  4. The scheme will only succeed ________ local residents are consulted from the start. (provided that)
  5. Many parents limit screen time ________ their children develop healthier habits. (so that)
  6. Congestion charges were introduced ________ reduce traffic in the city centre. (in order to)
  7. ________ demand for housing continues to rise, prices are unlikely to fall in the near future. (Since)
