Test Format

Format & Key Rules

The Basics

  • Recommended time: 40 minutes
  • Minimum word count: 250 words (aim for 270–300)
  • Worth 67% of your total writing marks
  • It is a formal academic essay
  • You are asked to respond to a point of view, argument, or problem

Critical Rules

  • Always write a conclusion — unlike Task 1
  • Do NOT use informal language or contractions (I'm / don't)
  • Do NOT begin sentences with "And" or "But"
  • Do NOT use bullet points — write in paragraphs
  • Always address every part of the question
  • Stick to your position — don't contradict yourself
Task 2 vs Task 1 — Key Difference Task 1 is a report with an overview (no conclusion). Task 2 is an essay with an introduction, body paragraphs, and a conclusion. Task 2 carries twice the weight — prioritise it if you are short on time.

Essay Types

The Four Essay Types

You must identify the essay type before you write — it determines your structure and approach. Misreading the question is the most common Task 2 mistake.

1. Opinion Essay

Trigger phrases: "To what extent do you agree or disagree?" / "Do you agree or disagree?"

You must give YOUR clear opinion and defend it throughout the essay. Do not sit on the fence.

Structure: Intro (position) → BP1 → BP2 → Conclusion (restate position)

2. Discussion Essay

Trigger phrases: "Discuss both views and give your own opinion." / "Some people think… Others believe…"

Present BOTH sides of the argument fairly, then state your own view.

Structure: Intro → BP1 (View A) → BP2 (View B) → Conclusion (your opinion)

3. Problem / Solution Essay

Trigger phrases: "What are the causes of this? What solutions can you suggest?" / "What problems does this create and how can they be addressed?"

Identify causes/problems and propose realistic solutions.

Structure: Intro → BP1 (Causes/Problems) → BP2 (Solutions) → Conclusion

4. Two-Part Question Essay

Trigger phrases: Two direct questions, e.g. "Why is this? Is this a positive or negative development?"

Answer both questions — one per body paragraph. Missing either question is penalised heavily.

Structure: Intro → BP1 (Answer Q1) → BP2 (Answer Q2) → Conclusion

How to Identify the Essay Type

  1. Read the question carefully — underline the key instruction.
  2. Count the questions — if there are two, it is a Two-Part Essay.
  3. Look for "agree or disagree" → Opinion; "both views" → Discussion; "causes and solutions" → Problem/Solution.
  4. If still unsure: default to a Discussion or Opinion structure, which works for most prompts.
⚠ "Discuss" alone does NOT mean Discussion Essay — check for "both views" ⚠ Missing one part of a Two-Part Question is a major Task Response error

Marking Criteria

How You Are Scored

25%
Task Response

Did you answer all parts of the question? Is your position clear throughout?

25%
Coherence & Cohesion

Is the essay well-organised? Do paragraphs flow logically with clear linking?

25%
Lexical Resource

Do you use a wide range of vocabulary accurately and with natural flexibility?

25%
Grammatical Range & Accuracy

Do you use complex sentences, mixed structures, and few errors?

Common Reasons for Low Scores

  • Task Response: Giving a vague position, missing part of the question, going off-topic
  • Coherence: No clear paragraph structure, random ordering of ideas, misusing linking words
  • Lexical Resource: Repeating basic vocabulary, using words incorrectly, spelling errors
  • Grammar: Run-on sentences, subject-verb errors, mixing up tenses
⚠ Repeating keywords from the question is not "good vocabulary" — paraphrase

Essay Structure

The 4-Paragraph Essay Structure

Para 1
Introduction (2–3 sentences)

Paraphrase the question topic. State your position or indicate what the essay will cover. Do NOT list your arguments in detail here — save them for the body.

Para 2
Body Paragraph 1 (5–7 sentences)

First main point. Topic sentence → Explanation → Example → Link back to question. Use TEE structure (see Section 5).

Para 3
Body Paragraph 2 (5–7 sentences)

Second main point. Same TEE structure. If Discussion Essay, present the opposing view here. If Opinion Essay, present your second supporting argument.

Para 4
Conclusion (2–3 sentences)

Restate your position using different words. Summarise the key points. Do NOT introduce new ideas or arguments in the conclusion.

Writing a Strong Introduction

Your introduction should do two things: (1) paraphrase the topic, (2) state your position (Opinion) or indicate the scope (Discussion/Problem-Solution).

✗ Weak Introduction
"Nowadays, many people think that the internet is good. Others think it is bad. In this essay I will discuss this topic."

Vague, no position, tells the reader nothing useful.

✓ Strong Introduction
"The proliferation of internet access over the past two decades has transformed nearly every aspect of daily life. While proponents argue that this has been an unambiguously positive development, I believe its benefits must be weighed carefully against significant social and psychological drawbacks."

Paraphrased, clear position stated, sophisticated vocabulary.

Writing a Strong Conclusion

  • Begin with: "In conclusion," / "To conclude," / "In summary,"
  • Restate your position using different language from the introduction
  • Summarise the key supporting ideas in one sentence
  • Optional: end with a forward-looking statement
⚠ Do NOT use "In conclusion, I have discussed…" — this adds no value ⚠ Do NOT introduce new arguments in the conclusion

Body Paragraphs

The TEE Structure

Each body paragraph should follow the TEE structure: Topic sentence → Explanation → Example. This keeps paragraphs focused, logical, and well-developed.

T
Topic Sentence — State your main point clearly

"One significant advantage of remote working is the improvement in work-life balance."

E
Explanation — Develop and justify the point (2–3 sentences)

"When employees are no longer required to commute, they regain several hours each week that can be dedicated to family, leisure, or health. This reduction in daily stress has been shown to increase overall productivity and job satisfaction."

E
Example — Provide a specific illustration

"A 2021 Stanford study, for instance, found that remote workers were 13% more productive than their office-based counterparts, largely attributed to fewer interruptions and reduced fatigue from commuting."

Useful Topic Sentence Starters

One of the main reasons… A significant advantage of… Perhaps the most compelling argument is… On the other hand,… Those who oppose this view argue that… Another key consideration is… It is also worth noting that… Furthermore,…

Useful Example Phrases

For example, For instance, A clear illustration of this is… This can be seen in… Take [country/case] as an example: Research has shown that… Studies suggest that…

Language

Key Essay Language

Linking — Addition

  • Furthermore, / Moreover, / In addition,
  • Not only… but also…
  • What is more,
  • On top of this,

Linking — Contrast

  • However, / Nevertheless, / Nonetheless,
  • Despite this, / In spite of this,
  • On the other hand,
  • While… / Whereas…

Linking — Cause & Effect

  • As a result, / Consequently, / Therefore,
  • This leads to / This results in…
  • Due to / Owing to
  • As a consequence of…

Hedging & Qualifying

  • It could be argued that…
  • It is widely believed that…
  • To a certain extent…
  • In many respects… / In some cases…
