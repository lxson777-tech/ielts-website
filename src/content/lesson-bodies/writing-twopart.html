Two-Part Questions

What is it?

The prompt states a situation and then asks direct questions, most often two, for example: "Why is this happening? Is it a positive or negative development?" Every question carries equal weight, and all of them must be answered fully. This type is really "Direct Question" essays, and the number of questions can vary. (The four-paragraph method itself is covered in How to Answer Task 2. This lesson is about adapting it here.)

Typical Question Pairs

  • What are the reasons for this? Is it a positive or negative development?
  • Why do people think this? Do you agree?
  • What are the causes? What effects does it have on society?
  • A question often smuggles in an opinion requirement: if it asks positive/negative or do-you-agree, you must take a position, not just describe.

How Many Questions? Adjust Your Paragraphs

QuestionsHow commonParagraph plan
One questionRare. Usually phrased as a single direct question with no second partStill four paragraphs: split your answer into two distinct angles or reasons, one per body paragraph, so each is properly developed rather than repeating the same point twice
Two questionsThe standard, most common formOne body paragraph per question. The default plan below
Three questionsOccasional, usually a chain like "What causes this? What effects does it have? What can be done?"Either five paragraphs (intro + one body per question + conclusion), or merge the two most closely related questions into one paragraph and give the third its own. Never compress all three into two paragraphs by rushing

Structure for the Standard Two-Question Form

  1. Introduction: paraphrase the situation + answer both questions in miniature: "This trend is driven mainly by X and Y, and I regard it as a broadly positive development."
  2. Body 1 = Question 1: answer it completely, with explanation and example.
  3. Body 2 = Question 2: answer it completely. Give it the same length and effort as Body 1.
  4. Conclusion: both answers restated in one or two sentences.

One question per body paragraph keeps Coherence clean and makes it impossible for the examiner to think you forgot half the task.

JobLanguage

Example question

Increasing numbers of people are choosing to live alone. What are the reasons for this trend? Is it a positive or negative development?
Show model introduction & plan

In many countries, solo living has shifted from a rarity to a common life choice. This essay argues that the trend stems chiefly from rising incomes and changing attitudes to marriage, and that despite concerns about loneliness it is, on balance, a positive development.

Plan: Body 1 answers "reasons" (affordability; independence valued over tradition). Body 2 answers "positive or negative" (position: positive. Autonomy and later, more deliberate partnerships; concede the loneliness risk). Conclusion restates both.


  • Introduction answers both questions in miniature ✓
  • One body paragraph per question, equal weight ✓
  • The opinion question gets a real position, with a concession ✓

Common Mistakes

⚠ Spending 80% of the essay on question 1 and a rushed sentence on question 2 ⚠ Answering "positive or negative?" with a list of both and no verdict ⚠ Merging every question into one muddled paragraph, whatever the count ⚠ A three-question prompt squeezed into two thin paragraphs ⚠ Introduction that only paraphrases and previews nothing
