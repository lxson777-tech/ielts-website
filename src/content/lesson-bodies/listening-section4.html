Academic Lecture

What is it?

  • One speaker giving a university-style lecture or academic talk
  • Examples: a lecture on climate change, urban development, animal behaviour, psychology
  • Typical question types: note / summary completion, sentence completion
  • Dense academic language, fast pace, and no pause in the middle, unlike other sections

Predicting the Answers

  • Read the notes in the preview and decide the word type each gap needs: noun? verb? adjective? Mark it N/V/Adj
  • The completed sentence must be grammatical, which narrows the possibilities enormously
  • Answers follow the order of the recording
  • Gaps usually take concrete content words straight from the lecture, so never paraphrase them yourself

Strategy

  1. Use the notes' headings as a route map: the lecture moves through them in order.
  2. Listen for signpost language: "Now I'd like to…", "Moving on to…", "Turning to…", "Finally…". Each one tells you the lecture has reached the next gap.
  3. Words like however, but, in fact flag the important, examinable statements.
  4. Write the exact word you hear, respecting the word limit; check plurals and spelling in the transfer time.
Simulation: Lecture Extract on Urban Beekeeping (read at a steady pace, as if listening)

Lecturer: Today I want to look at the rise of urban beekeeping. Twenty years ago, keeping hives in cities was seen as eccentric; today, rooftop hives are found on hotels, banks and even railway stations. The obvious question is why bees, which we associate with the countryside, are thriving in cities. The answer is largely about variety: while modern farmland is often dominated by a single crop, urban parks and gardens offer a remarkable diversity of flowering plants, which extends the foraging season. City bees are also exposed to far fewer pesticides than their rural cousins. There are challenges, of course. The most serious is density: when too many hives compete in one neighbourhood, there is simply not enough nectar to go around, and colonies weaken. For this reason, several cities have now introduced a registration system for new hives. Interestingly, the honey itself has become a selling point: because it reflects the local mix of plants, urban honey has a distinctive flavour that commands premium prices at farmers' markets.

Complete the notes (ONE WORD ONLY for each answer)

  1. Urban parks and gardens offer a wide _______________ of flowering plants.
  2. This variety extends the _______________ season for city bees.
  3. City bees encounter fewer _______________ than rural bees.
  4. The most serious problem for urban hives is _______________.
  5. Some cities now require _______________ of new hives.
  6. Urban honey's distinctive _______________ allows it to sell at premium prices.
Show Answers
  1. diversity (the notes already use "wide", so "variety" also accepted)
  2. foraging
  3. pesticides
  4. density
  5. registration
  6. flavour
